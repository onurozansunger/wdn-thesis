\noindent
\textbf{Overview.} This project builds a graph neural network that detects
sensor-level cyber-attacks on physics-constrained infrastructure, and asks
whether one detector can serve very different domains. \emph{Part~1}
develops and validates the method on water distribution networks;
\emph{Part~2} applies the identical method, unchanged, to an electrical
grid and a road-traffic network. The detector is an \emph{interpretable
cascade}: a fast router ranks a set of attack-specialised experts, and a
label-free feedback check accepts the chosen expert or re-routes. Two
questions drive the analysis --- how accurately can each attack be
detected, and \emph{why} does the hardest one (replay) behave as it does.

%===============================================================
\section{Problem and threat model}

An infrastructure network is a graph $G=(V,E)$: nodes are metered points
(junctions, buses, road sensors) carrying a scalar time-series, edges are
the links carrying a flow. Sensing is realistic --- roughly \textbf{50\% of
readings are missing} at any step and all readings carry observation noise.
An attacker compromises a subset of sensors and falsifies their reported
values. Five attack families are considered, spanning the standard
knowledge/capability spectrum (Table~\ref{tab:threat}). Per snapshot the
detector must (i)~\emph{reconstruct} the true value at every node and
(ii)~\emph{flag} which sensors are compromised.

\section{Method}

\textbf{Experts.} Six experts, one per attack class plus a clean class,
each a \textbf{GraphSAGE\,$\to$\,GRU} network over a 6-step sliding window
with four heads (pressure and flow reconstruction, pressure and flow
anomaly). Training combines a reconstruction loss, a per-sensor anomaly
loss, a router cross-entropy with a load-balancing term, a
mass-conservation penalty $\lVert B\mathbf{q}\rVert^2$ that forbids
physically impossible reconstructions, and --- crucially ---
\emph{direct expert supervision}: every expert is trained on its own attack
class regardless of where the router sent the window, so a systematically
mis-routed class never starves for gradient. The full model is 377\,K
parameters (Table~\ref{tab:cfg}).

\textbf{Cascade routing.} Rather than blending all experts opaquely, the
router \emph{ranks} them; the top-ranked expert is run and scored by a
\emph{label-free} feedback signal
\[
  s_k = \big\lVert \hat{p}_k - p\big\rVert_{\text{clean}}
        \;+\; \big\lVert B\hat{\mathbf q}_k\big\rVert^2 ,
\]
combining reconstruction consistency on the sensors the expert itself calls
clean with a physics residual, standardised across experts so neither term
dominates. On negative feedback the cascade re-routes to the next-ranked
expert, restricted to the router's \textbf{top-2} candidates for stability.
Every prediction therefore records \emph{which} expert ran and \emph{why}
it was kept or rejected --- an audit trail a soft mixture cannot give.

\textbf{Decision threshold.} The per-sensor anomaly score is thresholded to
a decision; the threshold is \emph{calibrated on validation} rather than
fixed at $0.5$, which matters once per-node normalisation shifts the score
distribution (Section~4).

%===============================================================
\section{Experimental setup}

Each domain is generated by a physics simulator, corrupted by the same
five-attack pipeline, and split 70/15/15 into train/validation/test by
scenario. Water uses EPANET on the Modena network (272 nodes, 317 pipes);
power uses \textsc{pandapower} load-flow on the IEEE 118-bus system;
traffic uses a 200-sensor road graph with rush-hour speed dynamics and
spatial diffusion. Every configuration is run over \textbf{5 seeds};
figures report the mean and the seed spread. Metrics are per-sensor anomaly
F1 and AUROC, reported overall and per attack class. Ranking quality
(AUROC) and thresholded quality (F1) are distinguished throughout, because
their gap is the crux of the replay analysis. The model and training
configuration are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:cfg}.

\section{Part 1 --- Water distribution (Modena)}

\textbf{Routing.} Over 5 seeds the cascade reaches F1 $0.772\pm0.017$,
matching the oracle ceiling ($0.769$, the best any hard routing could
achieve) and within a point of a full soft blend ($0.789$). The experts are
similar on this network, so the interpretable cascade is obtained at
negligible accuracy cost, and restricting re-routing to the router's top-2
keeps it stable across every seed (Figure~\ref{fig:routing}).

\textbf{Detection.} The detector is strong and reproducible
(Table~\ref{tab:full}; overall F1 $0.833$, AUROC $0.979$). Four of the five
attack families are detected reliably; the per-attack precision-recall
curves (Figure~\ref{fig:pr}) show random and targeted attacks are almost
perfectly separable, stealthy and noise nearly so, and only replay
collapses.

\textbf{Router diagnostic.} Direct measurement confirms a routing failure
mode: on Modena the router is healthy (96\% accuracy), but on the larger
Net3 topology it sends \textbf{77\% of stealthy windows to the replay
expert}, because both classes look smooth and 97 nodes give too little
context to separate them. The cascade's feedback and the direct expert
supervision are designed to contain exactly this case.

\textbf{Recovering replay.} A per-sensor normalisation --- rescaling each
sensor by its own temporal variance --- amplifies the tiny per-node
variation a replay lacks. This lifts replay \emph{ranking} from below chance
to AUROC $0.79$ while overall F1 holds (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}); it is the
single largest contribution to replay, and the supervisors' hypothesis that
``water values sit too close together'' is thereby confirmed
quantitatively.

\textbf{Why replay still underperforms.} Even after normalisation, replay
F1 stays low, and Figure~\ref{fig:replaywhy} explains why with direct
evidence. The anomaly scores of replayed and genuine sensors \emph{overlap}
(left): the ranking is informative (AUROC $0.75$) but no single threshold
separates the two, so the best achievable F1 is $0.41$ (right). The cause
is physical --- a replay is a verbatim copy of a past reading, so the only
distinguishing cue is the fresh observation noise a genuine reading has and
a copy lacks. This is an \emph{information limit}, not a training defect: a
direct exact-lag-match detector, built specifically to catch verbatim
copies, reaches only AUROC $0.62$, because with 50\% missing data and
replays that reference values outside the 6-step window the exact match is
often simply not observable.

\section{Part 2 --- Cross-domain generalisation}

The detector, training script and corruption pipeline are kept
\emph{unchanged}; only a per-domain data adapter is written (bus voltage
and Kirchhoff's current law for the grid; sensor speed and flow
conservation for the road network). Detection quality then lands in a tight
band across all three physical systems (Table~\ref{tab:full}; F1
$0.82$--$0.89$), which is evidence the method is a general detector for
attacks on sensor graphs rather than a water-specific construction.

\textbf{The normalisation fix generalises.} Figure~\ref{fig:gen} shows the
replay ranking per domain. The global-normalisation baseline (grey) already
tracks \emph{signal speed}: replay is hardest in slow-moving water ($0.46$)
and trivial in fast-moving traffic ($0.97$), consistent with the
intra-scenario variation spanning seven orders of magnitude
(Table~\ref{tab:signal}). Per-node normalisation (purple) lifts replay
\emph{most where it is hardest} (water $0.46\!\to\!0.79$, power
$0.66\!\to\!0.90$) and is unnecessary where replay is already easy. The fix
is therefore principled and targeted, not a domain-specific hack.

\textbf{The difficulty profile inverts.} The hardest attack class flips
between domains: water and power find stealthy easy and replay hard,
whereas traffic is the mirror image (Table~\ref{tab:full}) --- a slow drift
in traffic speed blends into natural congestion, while a verbatim replay of
a fast-changing speed reading stands out immediately. Which attacks are
detectable is decided by the domain's signal dynamics, not by the model.

\section{Discussion, limitations and next steps}

Three results stand out. First, an \emph{interpretable} routing procedure
can match an opaque mixture-of-experts at negligible cost, which is
valuable in a safety-critical setting where an operator must understand why
a sensor was flagged. Second, a simple, physically-motivated normalisation
recovers most of the replay signal, and does so consistently across
domains. Third, the residual replay gap is characterised, with direct
evidence, as an information limit set by signal dynamics --- a statement
that holds for any detector, not just this one.

\emph{Limitations.} The traffic dataset is synthetic (the power grid is a
standard benchmark and the water network is real); a real traffic benchmark
such as METR-LA is the natural validation. The cascade matches but does not
exceed the soft blend on Modena because the experts are similar there;
networks with more heterogeneous experts should let the interpretable
re-routing also \emph{gain} accuracy. Replay remains fundamentally limited
on slow-signal domains.

\emph{Next steps.} (1)~Apply the cascade to networks whose experts differ
more. (2)~Formalise the cross-domain study as a bounded-attacker robustness
statement. (3)~Extend the threat model toward the smart-grid FDIA and
intelligent-transport-security literature and consolidate the three domains
into a single evaluation for a target venue.

\section{Reproducibility}

Every number in this report is a mean over 5 independent seeds per
configuration. Figure~\ref{fig:repro} shows the two facets of that
reproducibility. \emph{Left:} on the per-node water models the validation
F1 converges within roughly 30 epochs and the 5-seed band is narrow
throughout training. \emph{Right:} the final test F1 for all six
configurations (three domains $\times$ two normalisation modes) is tightly
clustered around its mean, with no outlier seed. The complete set of
figures and machine-readable tables behind this report --- per-attack
precision-recall, learning curves, seed variance, the ablation, the router
confusion matrices and the signal statistics --- is provided in the
accompanying \texttt{results\_package/} directory, and the experiments are
reproduced by the scripts under \texttt{scripts/} from the released code.
